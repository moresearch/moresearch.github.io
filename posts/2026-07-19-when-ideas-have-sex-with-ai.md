In 2010, Matt Ridley stood on a TED stage and gave human progress one of its most memorable metaphors: **ideas have sex.**

His argument was simple and devastating. No single person on Earth knows how to make a pencil — let alone a computer mouse. Someone knows how to drill for oil. Someone knows how to injection-mold plastic. Someone knows how to assemble the circuit board. But nobody holds the whole blueprint in their head. The things we build are the offspring of ideas meeting other ideas, across minds, across generations, across continents.

That's how we got from the Acheulean hand axe — unchanged for 30,000 generations — to a smartphone that's obsolete in three years. The difference isn't smarter brains. The difference is that in the modern world, **ideas keep bumping into each other.**

> Progress is combinatorial. Intelligence is collective. Exchange is the engine.

Ridley's evidence was Tasmania: cut off by rising seas 10,000 years ago, a population of 4,000 was too small to sustain specialization. They didn't just stop advancing — they *lost* technologies. Bone tools. Fishing nets. Stitching. Isolation doesn't freeze progress. It reverses it.

## Enter the recombination engine

For most of history, ideas could only meet inside human minds. You had to read the paper, hear the lecture, share the trade route, learn the craft. The collision rate was bottlenecked by how fast people could talk to each other.

The internet changed the transmission speed. But it didn't change the recombination step — the actual synthesis still happened inside a human brain, one idea at a time.

Generative AI is the first technology that doesn't just transmit ideas. **It recombines them.**

A large language model is not a database. It doesn't retrieve. It recombines. Train it on the collective output of humanity — every paper, every poem, every Stack Overflow thread, every forgotten blog post from 2003 — and it doesn't memorize. It learns the latent space *between* ideas. Give it a prompt and it finds paths through that space that no human has walked.

This is not like previous technologies. The printing press copied ideas. The telegraph transmitted them. The internet indexed them. **Generative AI mates them.**

## What this means for the collective brain

Ridley's central claim was that progress depends on the collective brain — the network of human minds exchanging and recombining ideas. If he's right, then generative AI is the most significant acceleration of the collective brain since language itself.

Think about it this way. Before language, ideas died with the individual. After language, ideas could transmit across minds within a generation. After writing, they could transmit across generations. After the printing press, they could scale. After the internet, they could transmit globally at zero cost. After generative AI, **they can recombine without a human in the loop.**

That last step is the one that matters. Every previous technology was a better pipe for moving ideas between people. This one is a new participant in the recombination process itself. It's not a pipe. It's a collaborator.

A researcher asks an AI to explore the space between two papers nobody had connected. A designer prompts for a synthesis of art deco and biophilic architecture. A programmer describes a problem and the model proposes a solution pattern that combines three techniques from three different languages, none of which the programmer knows. Every one of these is ideas having sex — faster and across more domains than any human mind could manage.

## The optimistic reading

Ridley ended his talk as an optimist. He argued that as long as ideas can meet and mate, living standards rise. Wars, recessions, and disasters slow the curve but don't flatten it. The underlying engine — specialization plus exchange — is too powerful to stop.

The optimistic reading of generative AI goes further. If recombination is the engine, then adding a recombination catalyst makes the engine run hotter. The combinatorial space of ideas is unimaginably vast. Most possible recombinations have never been tried — not because they're worthless, but because the human mind-hours didn't exist to try them. AI changes that math.

> The bottleneck was never the supply of ideas. It was the recombination rate. That rate just changed.

This doesn't mean AI replaces human creativity. It means AI expands the search surface. Humans still ask the questions, set the direction, apply the taste, make the judgment. But now they can explore a thousand combinatorial directions in the time it used to take to explore one. That's not replacement. That's leverage.

## The caution

There is a caution, and it's Ridley's own.

If isolation reverses progress — if Tasmania lost technologies because its network was too small — then the risk of generative AI is not that it's too powerful. It's that it becomes **the only recombination engine anyone uses.**

When every idea is filtered through the same model, the diversity of recombination paths collapses. The model has a distribution. It has biases baked into its training. If we stop exchanging ideas with *each other* and only exchange them with the model, the collective brain shrinks to the size of a latent space.

The internet promised to connect everyone to everyone. In practice, it funneled attention through a handful of platforms. Generative AI risks a similar collapse: all roads leading through the same few models, trained on the same corpus, optimized for the same objective function.

> The antidote is the same thing Ridley prescribed: more exchange. More independent thinking. More weird, unoptimized, human-to-human recombination that no model can predict.

## The real question

Ridley's metaphor gave us a way to see progress not as a march of geniuses but as a network of minds. The question generative AI poses is not "will machines get smarter than us?" That's the wrong frame. The real question is: **will the collective brain expand or contract?**

If we use AI as a recombination partner while keeping the human network alive and diverse, the collective brain gets larger than it has ever been. Every human mind connected to every other, plus a synthesis engine operating at a scale we can barely imagine. That's not a replacement of humanity's greatest asset. That's its amplification.

If we let the models become the only place ideas meet — if we outsource recombination to a few companies running a few models on a few training distributions — we've built Tasmania at planetary scale. A small network, looking at itself, slowly forgetting what it used to know.

The technology is here. Whether it expands the collective brain or shrinks it is not a question about the technology. It's a question about us.
